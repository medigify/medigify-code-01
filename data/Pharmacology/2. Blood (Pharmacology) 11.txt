Question: A 23-year-old pregnant lady presented with Hb 10 g/dL (normal 12–16 g/dL), macrocytic anemia, an increased serum concentration of transferrin, and a normal serum concentration of vitamin B12. The most likely cause of her anemia is deficiency of which of the following:
Options:
a) Cobalamin
b) Iron
c) Folic acid
d) Intrinsic factor
Correct Option:
Ans. Folic acid
Explanation: During pregnancy, there is a high fetal demand for folate to support rapid DNA synthesis. In contrast, if the anemia is normocytic or microcytic, it is typically treated with ferrous sulfate tablets to address the increased iron requirements of both the mother and the developing fetus. Iron deficiency would result in microcytic cells rather than macrocytic ones.

Question: Toxicity associated with acute iron poisoning usually includes which of the following:
Options:
a) Dizziness, hypertension, and cerebral hemorrhage
b) Hyperthermia, delirium, and coma
c) Hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, and seizures
d) Necrotizing gastroenteritis, shock, and metabolic acidosis
Correct Option:
Ans. Necrotizing gastroenteritis, shock, and metabolic acidosis
Explanation: Acute iron poisoning is a medical emergency characterized by necrotizing gastroenteritis, shock, and metabolic acidosis resulting from the direct corrosive action of iron on the gut and its interference with cellular respiration.

Options:
a) Allergic reactions
b) Cancer recurrence
c) Hypoxia

Correct Option:
Ans. Systemic infection
Explanation: Filgrastim stimulates the bone marrow to produce and release neutrophils. Question: The child ingested iron-containing supplements in large amount & presented with acute iron poisoning. What immediate treatment is necessary? Correction of acid-base and electrolyte abnormalities and:
Options:
a) Activated charcoal
b) Oral deferasirox
c) Parenteral deferoxamine
d) Parenteral dantrolene
Correct Option:
Ans. Parenteral deferoxamine
Explanation: This agent is a specific iron-chelating drug that binds to ferric iron in the blood to form ferrioxamine, which is then safely excreted by the kidneys. Activated charcoal is ineffective because it does not bind well to metals like iron, and other agents like dantrolene are used for entirely different conditions like malignant hyperthermia.

Question: Treatment of Hemochromatosis due to frequent blood transfusion is:
Options:
a) Parenteral deferoxamine
b) Activated charcoal
c) Iron dextran
d) Ferumoxytol
Correct Option:
Ans. Parenteral deferoxamine
Explanation: Patients requiring frequent blood transfusions are at high risk for iron overload (hemochromatosis) because the body has no physiological mechanism to excrete the excess iron provided by the donor cells. Parenteral deferoxamine is the established treatment because it effectively chelates the systemic iron deposits and promotes their removal through the urine. Using iron supplements like iron dextran or ferumoxytol would be dangerous in this scenario as they would further exacerbate the toxic iron accumulation.

Question: Contraindications of iron therapy is:
Options:
a) Pregnancy
b) Microcytic hypochromic anemia
c) Blood loss anemia
d) Hemolytic anemia
Correct Option:
Ans. Hemolytic anemia
Explanation: Iron therapy is strictly contraindicated in hemolytic anemia. This is because the premature destruction of red blood cells releases their internal iron back into the systemic circulation, meaning the body is already saturated with recycled iron. Providing supplemental iron in this state can lead to rapid iron toxicity and organ damage.

Question: Which of the following is a granulocyte colony stimulating factor:
Options:
a) Erythropoietin
b) Filgrastim (G-CSF)
c) Oprelvekin (interleukin-11)
d) Sargramostim
Correct Option:
Ans. Filgrastim (G-CSF)
Explanation: Filgrastim is the clinical name for recombinant human granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF). It works specifically to increase the production of neutrophils, a type of white blood cell, in the bone marrow. Other options like sargramostim are GM-CSF (affecting both granulocytes and macrophages), and oprelvekin is an interleukin that specifically targets platelet production rather than granulocytes.

Question: A 45-year-old male stomach cancer patient underwent tumor removal surgery. After surgery, he developed megaloblastic anemia. His anemia is caused by a deficiency of X and can be treated with Y:
Options:
a) X = intrinsic factor; Y = folic acid
b) X = intrinsic factor; Y = vitamin B12
c) X = extrinsic factor; Y = parenteral iron
d) X = extrinsic factor; Y = sargramostim
Correct Option:
Ans. X = intrinsic factor; Y = vitamin B12
Explanation: Patients who undergo surgical removal of the stomach (gastrectomy) lose the parietal cells that secrete intrinsic factor. Since intrinsic factor is essential for the absorption of vitamin B12 in the small intestine, its absence leads to a specific type of megaloblastic anemia. This condition is treated by bypassing the GI tract with parenteral Vitamin B12.
