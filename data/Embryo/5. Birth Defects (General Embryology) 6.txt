Question: Webbed neck & gonadal dysgenesis are the characteristic findings of:
Options:
a) Turner syndrome
b) Down syndrome
c) Klinefelter syndrome
d) Edward syndrome
Correct Option:
Ans. Turner syndrome
Explanation: Turner syndrome, a 45, X karyotype, is the only monosomy compatible with life. 98% of all fetuses with the syndrome are spontaneously aborted. The few that survive are unmistakably female in appearance and are characterized by the absence of ovaries (gonadal dysgenesis) and short stature. Other common associated abnormalities are webbed neck, lymphedema of the extremities, skeletal deformities, and a broad chest with widely spaced nipples.

Question: Mental deficiency, retarded growth, slanting eyes, small ears, neural tube defects & cardiac defects are the characteristic findings in:
Options:
a) Trisomy 13
b) Trisomy 18
c) Trisomy 21
d) Turner syndrome
Correct Option:
Ans. Trisomy 21
Explanation: Also known as down syndrome, is caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21. Features include growth retardation, intellectual disability, craniofacial abnormalities, including upward slanting eyes, epicanthal folds (extra skin folds at the medial corners of the eyes), flat facies and small eyes: cardiac defects and hypotonia.
95% is caused by meiotic nondisjunction and in 75% of these instances, this nondisjunction occurs during oocyte formation.
4% of cases are caused by unbalanced translocation while the last 1% by mitotic nondisjunction causing mosaicism.

Question: Deletion of short arm of chromosome 5 is known as:
Options:
a) Angelman syndrome
b) Cri-du-chat syndrome
c) Miller-Dieker syndrome
d) Prader-Willi syndrome
Correct Option:
Ans. Cri-du-chat syndrome
Explanation: Partial deletion of short arm of chromosome 5 causes cri-du-chat syndrome. Affected infants have a cat-like cry, microcephaly (small head), intellectual disability and congenital heart disease.

Question: Warfarin is contraindicated in pregnancy. Which of the following abnormalities are commonly associated with warfarin use in first trimester:
Options:
a) Dextrocardia
b) Nasal hypoplasia
c) Renal agenesis
d) Claw hand
Correct Option:
Ans. Nasal hypoplasia
Explanation: Warfarin crosses the placenta and interferes with vitamin K–dependent bone and cartilage formation in the fetus. When taken in the 1st trimester (especially weeks 6–9), it leads to characteristic defects like nasal hypoplasia (depressed nasal bridge).
