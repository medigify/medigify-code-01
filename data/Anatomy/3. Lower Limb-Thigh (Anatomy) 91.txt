Question: A patient has a rare injury where the accessory obturator nerve is compressed. Which function is likely to be impaired:
(a) Hip flexion and adduction
(b) Knee extension
(c) Thigh lateral rotation
(d) Ankle dorsiflexion
Correct Option:
Ans. Hip flexion and adduction
Explanation: The accessory obturator nerve (present in ~10% of cases) typically supplies the pectineus muscle. This "hybrid" muscle is located in the floor of the femoral triangle and assists in both flexing and adducting the hip, so its loss would subtly weaken both movements.

Question: During a posterior approach to the hip, a surgeon accidentally transects the sciatic nerve before it bifurcates. Which of the following functions will be lost first:
(a) Hip extension
(b) Knee flexion and foot dorsiflexion
(c) Hip adduction
(d) Hip abduction
Correct Option:
Ans. Knee flexion and foot dorsiflexion
Explanation: The sciatic nerve provides motor control to the hamstrings (knee flexors) and every muscle in the leg and foot. Transecting it before it splits causes immediate paralysis of all foot movements (like dorsiflexion) and significantly weakens the ability to flex the knee.

Question: A patient presents with weakness in lateral rotation of the thigh and inability to sit cross-legged after injury near the ischial tuberosity. Which small lateral rotator muscle is most likely affected:
(a) Quadratus femoris
(b) Obturator internus
(c) Gemellus superior
(d) Gemellus inferior
Correct Option:
Ans. Quadratus femoris
Explanation: The Quadratus femoris is a flat, rectangular muscle that originates from the lateral border of the ischial tuberosity. It is a powerful lateral rotator of the thigh, and its proximity to the tuberosity makes it highly vulnerable to injuries or fractures in that specific area.
